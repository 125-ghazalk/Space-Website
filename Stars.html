Constellations are groups of stars that make a "picture."
Constellations are often named after the thing they resemble, or an ancient mythological figure.

The Pleiades and Orion the Hunter

The most famous constellation is Orion, an ancient Greek mythological figure. His most defining feature is his belt, made up of 3 stars together closely in a line. Orion chases the Pleiades, which are a star cluster and in mythology, a group of women turned into stars by the god Zeus to escape Orion The Hunter. They are class B stars, which are the second hottest, brightest, and have a lot of energy.

Ursa Major

Ursa Major is one of the most well known constellations in the world. Another name for it is the Great Bear and we have known about this constellation since 2,000 years ago, although it didn't have a name yet. Well known and prominent constellations like Ursa Major have helped humans navigate directions for thousands of years as they are a constant in our sky.

Star types are determined by their luminosity, colour, and temperate. Star types are classified using a Hertzprung-Russell diagram.

Hertzprung-Russel Diagram

A Hertzprung-Russel diagram is a way to visualize how stars are classified. Most stars, including our Sun, are Main Sequence stars. Main Sequence stars are the most common type, and make up about 90% of all stars. these stars spend most of their lifetime in as a Main Sequence star, but the specific amount of time spent is based on its mass. High mass stars, instead of dying out like other stars, go through 3 other stages. Supergiant stars, once they become really hot, go through nuclear fusion in its core, until the element iron is formed. Once this state is reached, the star dies and goes supernova because of the core shrinking, getting hotter, until it collapses. It can then turn into a neutron star, or if the core is heavier, turn into a Black hole.
